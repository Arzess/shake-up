# Literature analysis

With the rise of electronic devices, the fields of human life have gone through an evolution, a positive evolution. It won’t be an exaggeration to say that society has undergone a digital revolution.  A wide range of gadgets have emerged and made their way into our daily lives. Mobile phones, and more importantly, mobile apps have become so ubiquitous that we would have a hard time imagining our day without using a calendar or a browser.

Naturally, nothing comes without a price. Developing an app is a feat requiring both a great understanding of your clientele and implementation that’s just as good. A flaw in logic or a design failure may cost thousands of dollars or even something that comes with a much higher price, if any – human lives. 

The health sector takes a substantial share of the application market.  To promote and facilitate a healthy lifestyle, experts design apps that address the nuances of nourishment, exercise, and habits. In this literature analysis, we focus specifically on the scope of our application – food labeling.

Food labeling is a double-edged sword; on one hand, it offers a comprehensive nutritional breakdown; on the other, it gets exponentially difficult for people to decide what is healthy and what they should skip. As a result, there was a surge in health-related apps to help make that choice easier than ever before. 

Based on the results of a Canadian study of user feedback on the FoodFlip© smartphone application, most participants preferred an application over an unclear nutritional facts table. The participants found the app easy to use, trustworthy, and goal-serving. The app implemented a nutritional rundown in the form of a traffic light and warning labels, as well as star ratings.

The acceptance and trustworthiness of such nutrition and fitness apps are important questions necessary for the development of the app. A 2022 study, using the MMAT and AXIS appraisal tools, investigated how middle-aged and older adults perceive those apps. Those age groups are arguably the ones with the highest rates of age-related diseases. According to the study, changing the very behavioral patterns that put them at risk and adopting health-promoting ones may reduce the onset of age-related diseases.

Even though the general rate of older people who use apps has increased, the number of non-users stays stable. It was referenced from other studies that long-term engagement was “rather modest”, though it is worth noting that this concerned younger populations. Older age groups are less likely to use those kinds of apps, and it’s worth looking into their acceptance among them. It was done by incorporating tracking not only quantitative metrics such as frequency of use but also empirical ones like Perceived usefulness or Ease of use.

It was concluded that among older age groups, usage rates were the lowest due to less perceived usefulness, lack of ease of use, and greater privacy and trustworthiness concerns. 

Fitness apps, on the contrary, were deemed somewhat more acceptable, most likely due to a lack of perceived usefulness. Factors such as “easy and automatic handling and simple tracking“ determined the longevity of the app usage. Food tracking and food logging were found to be less usable because of detailed entries and a great cognitive load. 

According to the study, men were found to use fitness apps more often, whereas women used nutritional apps more on average. Overall, because those age groups are less likely to use those fitness or nutritional apps, it’s worth considering their distinct needs in early development.


Another study in 2024 compared 10 prominent mHealth apps in the United States across their energy consumption levels on users’ personal mobile devices. It was noted that regular app use significantly increased energy consumption due to the growing complexity of app functionality. Moreover, cross-platform frameworks considerably multiplied energy consumption compared to native apps. 

Features that were tested during the study were primarily basic functions such as logging meals or connecting wearables to the app. The research, however, also highlighted how the use of notifications and alerts played a significant role in energy consumption. After taking a mean, it was concluded that using an mHealth app for just an hour a day took 15% to 20% of the phone’s battery.

More importantly, apps designed to ensure the most engagement via, for example, fun gamification features were seen to drain the battery the hardest.

Design is a crucial part of any app. With careful planning and a deep, forehand analysis, it is very well possible to develop an app worth using. An American case study in 2018 used formative research to raise and resolve design issues in developing a nutritional app. For this study, an app was created. This app provided recipes for low-income families to help them efficiently use their food resources without prior knowledge of the ingredients.

After analyzing user behavior and breaking down user feedback, it was found that separating the decisions on the main screen has lowered the cognitive load. The placement of the functions played a big role when users inadvertently pressed the wrong button either because it was close to the interface of the phone itself or because the “tap area” wasn’t big enough.

The way information was presented in the app was another testing criterion. Using 5-point scales was found “very helpful” and more insightful than plain text. Incorporating hand-drawn illustrations to accentuate information was found to be a better option than using photographs with more visual details because of their simplified graphics.

The study launched the app on Android first and then later developed the iOS version. This took more time and resources than they had expected. The takeaway was that the platform-choice nuance should be decided in the very early stages of app development to save time and resources.



### ShakeUp Takeaway

The analysis sheds light on nuances that must be considered during the early stages of development of a nutritional app. ShakeUp must thoughtfully implement user engagement methods to avoid unnecessary energy consumption. That entails crafting app features and user interactions in ways that naturally lead to less energy use, without sacrificing the quality of the user experience.

The app already wins over standard nutritional value tables due to a clearer and interactive representation. However, among older age groups, the usage rates are the lowest because of privacy concerns and a lack of ease of use. Hence, to satisfy those cohorts and ensure long-term enjoyment among them, the app must focus on the straightforwardness of task flows and clear steps. Moreover, ShakeUp must implicitly show why it’s useful.

Because this app is health-related, it must be possible to set personal boundaries for users, such as intakes that might be over or under the recommended daily values. 

Another step towards usability would be to use hand-drawn illustrations to support texts and use a smart and thoughtful placement and scale of the interface, either as a whole or in units.
