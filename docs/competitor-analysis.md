# Competitior analysis

<strong>Result of the analysis: </strong> <br>

None of the apps focus on any physical transformation; they are all terse, pragmatic, and have no room for playfulness, which our app places a high value on. All apps on the market focus heavily on logging food daily or providing pre-defined recipes. There’s not a single app on the market that allows the user to create their own drinks, learn about their nutritional value, compatibility with diets, recommended tailored daily intake, and smooth animations.

Some apps we’ve studied include functionality ShakeUp offers, but it’s either limited, not full, or complicated, which leads to user frustration and less app satisfaction.

The only app that is somewhat close to ShakeUp is Nutritional Facts Maker. However, it is outdated, dry, heavily data-driven, and is only available for iOS. Furthermore, ShakeUp stands out due to its free-of-charge, ad-free, and transparent policy, which some of our age groups value highly.


## Competitors

### 1. MyFitnessPal

MyFitnessPal is a comprehensive food and fitness tracker; it tracks macros, calories, food, and workouts.

#### Functions

- Creating a meal
    - Enter the name of the meal.
    - You can add a photo of it.
	- You can put directions for the meal in the form of an instruction list.
-	Creating food
    -	Enter food name and description
    -	Include servings
    -	Manually type in macros (all are optional, except for calories)
    -	If your information is unreasonable, the app states it.
-	Adding water
    -	Choose between different volume presets, including a custom input bottle.
    -	Click on the add button.
-	Logging food
    -	You can choose between All foods, Your created meals, Your recipes, and Foods.
    -	Additionally, to skip the long logging process, you can voice log it or scan a  barcode.
    -	You search for food in the search bar; you can filter out unapproved/unlicensed     entries.
    -	To add a specific food, you click on the plus button and get a notification.
    -	Additionally, if you click on an entry, you can see its food composition and    customize the serving.
    -	Alternatively, you can quick log food by including calories and specifying the meal type (“Breakfast”, “Lunch”, etc.). Macros, however, are not available for the free tier.

-	Creating a recipe
    -	Enter the name and serving description.
    -	There’s an option for the bulk import of the ingredients.
    -	After you’re finished, you see the overall description and nutritional breakdown    of your recipe.
    -	You can delete ingredients by selecting them and then deleting them in the top  corner (too much to do).

-	Browse recipes
    -	The recipes are sorted by categories such as “High Fiber”
    -	You see a list of 6 recipes that you can scroll through; the rest can be viewed     using the “View More” button above.
    -	You can bookmark them, and if the pictures are not loaded, you have placeholders. 
    -	Bookmarking, however, is not free and is only available with the premium    subscription.
    -	Each recipe has a quick nutritional breakdown that can be expanded. 
    -	It shows the number of servings, some common tags (e.g., “Dinner” or “Plant-Based   Protein”), the ingredients, and directions.

#### App flow

1. User opens the app and is greeted with a sign-up modal. There’s a gallery that provides a quick overview of the app features. 
    - The app asks for your name, sets goals, personal hurdles, medications, activity level, preferences, habits, and age/country, accompanied by personal feedback on each step.
    - It then asks for your measurements and follows up with your recommended daily calorie intake.
    - After the onboarding process is complete, the app briefly explains its basic functions. 
    - It proceeds to show that premium is free for only a week. There’s a possibility to avoid the free trial and use the basic version of the app.
    - You receive an email asking you to confirm your email.

2. After signing up, the app starts a tutorial of the core functionality, walking you through the logging process.
    -	You log your food and receive feedback; after that, the tutorial ends.

#### <i>Where it shines:</i>
-	The design is straightforward, minimalistic, and simple, following a similar approach ShakeUp has set.
-	You can include pictures of your meals.
-	There are simple 2d vector illustrations such as bottles and drinks.


#### <i>Features to improve:</i>
-	You must sign up or log in, which takes some time and saves your personal data that is explicitly said to be processed and transferred to other countries (unless you don’t tick that box).
-	Creating a meal is difficult and takes a lot of time.
-	The food composition is rather scarce and not complete. You cannot export it.
-	No allergen information.
-	Some features in the free version are limited, and those features are basic too, for example, bookmarking.
-	There are ads, though they aren’t annoying.
-	For every search, you must click on the search button on the keyboard, which adds to the cognitive load.
-	You can’t search for any foods offline, not even basic ones.

#### <i>Where ShakeUp wins:</i>
-	No registration needed; you can use the app right out of the gate.
-	Food composition is complete, and you can see your diet compatibility as well as allergens.
-	Every single feature is available for free and for anyone, which leads to less frustration and appeals to a broader range of clients.
-	Slightly edging out on friendliness of the design, animations, and sound effects.
-	The app is fully open-source, transparent, and lightweight.
-	Every task you do using the app is extremely simple, automatic, and requires zero thought.
-	Offline library with the most common foods.

#### <i>Where ShakeUp concedes:</i>
-	The app’s functionality is rather simpler compared to a functional giant such as MyFitnessPal. 
-	MyFitnessPal is already established on the market and has a good reputation among people, which threatens a possible oppression if the app grows.
-	The database isn’t as big and specific.
-	No scanning functionality.

#### <i>Takeaway:</i>
-	Include pictures of your creations for later organization in collections.
-	Use simple vector graphics for illustrating app functions.
-	Implement bulk import of the ingredients.

### 2. My Cocktail Bar

My Cocktail Bar is an app where you can browse or search through a recipe list to find a cocktail you want. All cocktails are organized into different categories. Additionally, you can create your own cocktails from the ingredients at hand.

#### App flow

1. Once you open the app, you are welcomed by a tutorial.
2. Users can browse an already pre-filled list of their cocktails.



#### <i>Where it shines:</i>
-	Clean tutorial.
-	All the drinks can be viewed without internet access.
-	Clear instructions and good descriptions of cocktails.
-	Option of adding your favorite drinks.
-	Great organization, tag system, and clear pictures.
-	Ability to filter entries.
-	Every recipe, either pre-defined or custom, can be edited.



#### <i>Features to improve:</i>
-	The design is amateur, bland, and lacking taste. It is robotic, blocky, inconsistent, and rather not user-friendly. Some of the functions are unclear at first. Lacks contrast in some places and looks unfinished. However, it is recognizable because it follows Material UI practices.
-	Too many actions on the screen. The user, especially an inexperienced one, can get lost quickly.
-	Creating an ingredient and a cocktail, which should be one of the core features, is unintuitive. It is hidden and can only be found by clicking on three dots in the top-right corner. 
-	The search doesn’t print the items out as normally expected (it creates a whole new modal with the results but should instead sort these on the screen).
-	The shopping list where you put items you want to buy couldn’t be found because of how unintuitive it is.
-	The app flow is basic. There aren’t levels of available functionality to the app.


#### <i>Where ShakeUp wins:</i>
-	Much better, user-friendly, animated design.
-	Provides more functionality.


#### <i>Takeaway:</i>

ShakeUp should implement a bookmarking feature, potentially shrinking user search times while adding foods to the mix. The image collection must be optional for the sole purpose of UX.

### 3. Samsung Food: Meal Planner

All-in-one app for recipe saving, meal planning, grocery shopping, and recipe sharing.

#### App flow

1.	User opens the app and logs in to their Samsung Account
2.	Then, the user is prompted to select the goals, special diet, and health data as well as activity level. 
3.	Upon finishing the onboarding process, the app suggests communities.
4.	Recipes that the user can try are shown; you can bookmark them or skip this step.
5.	It then suggests pre-made plans.
6.	User lands on the home page.


#### <i>Functions</i>

-	Browsing a recipe’s description
    -	You can bookmark it, add it to your shopping list, meal plan, or a collection, and share it.
    -	You see a brief description of the recipe that can be expanded. It shows basic ingredients tailored to the number of servings. You see preparation time and cook time. You can edit the recipe if you want, either manually or with AI.
    -	For every ingredient, you can see suggested alternatives.
    -	You can look up the instructions, but they are in an internal browser rather than plain text.
    -	You can see its Health Score, which is sometimes available and sometimes not, suggesting it’s user-based.
    -	If there is a health score, you can see the score itself, if it's “High”, “Medium” or “Low”, information about it, the nutrients that positively impacted it and nutrients that negatively impacted it.
    -	You can see the exact nutrition of it, showing basic criteria; however, it can be expanded to a more comprehensive list.
    -	Additionally, you can see the Glycemic Index of the recipe.

-	Browsing bookmarked recipes
    -	You can sort them by “Ingredients”, “Meal Type,” etc.
    -	There are collections; basic ones are seen right away, and the rest can be seen via the “See all” button.
    -	You can see your saved Meal Plans, the number of which is limited for basic users, recently viewed recipes, your own recipes, planned ones, and made ones.
    -   Furthermore, you can create your own custom collection.
    -	Recipes can be sorted in different ways such as by Relevance, A to Z, Newest, Oldest, etc. You can also explore new ones in the bookmark section.



#### <i>Where it shines:</i>
-	The design is exceptionally well-done. It is simple, highly intuitive, and follows the best design practices. However, some users note that the app structure is impractical. 
-	High compatibility with Samsung users. 
-	It is highly specific because it lets users select concrete shops. 
-	Clear Health Score system with readable nutritional ratings.
-	Great transparent pictures.
-	Great individualization (Diets, ingredient avoidance).
-	High flexibility (Alternative suggestions, editing recipes, converting units)




#### <i>Features to improve:</i>
-	According to user reviews, some basic functions can be unintuitive or not available. For example, it is not possible to see what ingredients the user has in the food list while browsing a recipe. 
-	No tutorial.
-	Some features are only available for Samsung Food+.
-	People don’t seem to like an abundance of information.
-	No custom servings like cups, tablespoons, etc.
-	Users report not liking the way the app switches to the internal browser instead of having plain information shown on the screen within the app.
-	Requires a Samsung account.
-	Data is processed outside the app.
-	The number of plans is limited



#### <i>Where ShakeUp wins:</i>
-	Onboarding tutorial
-	Every feature the user may need is implemented, easily accessible in a few clicks, and intuitive.
-	Highly customizable serving sizes. 
-	The app is stable and standalone.
-	Every app function is accessible.
-	No account required, compatible with every operating system.
-	Fully confidential.


#### <i>Where ShakeUp concedes:</i>

- There’s no communication between users. The app focuses on mixing ingredients and giving a nutritional breakdown. There’s no community.

#### <i>Takeaway:</i>

ShakeUp can draw inspiration from the design style used because of its simplicity and user-friendliness. The app can use a rating system for the most common nutrients, making it extremely simple to determine whether the composition is healthy.
We can generate or use license-free images with a transparent background to help users save less time processing the ingredients before adding them.

Any description must be kept short, avoiding unnecessary details. Everything must be kept within the app without opening the browser.

The user, upon creating a food mix, should be able to set its preparation and cooking times, if any are required. Additionally, it should be possible to see the glycemic index of the mix.

Add last accessed or viewed foods to optimize lookup and search times. Implement a suggestion system.
